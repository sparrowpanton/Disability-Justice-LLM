## Quick Orientation: What Are Training Pairs?

Training pairs (also called instruction-tuning data) are structured examples that teach an LLM how to respond. The most common format is the **Alpaca format**, which looks like this:

```json
{
  "instruction": "Reframe this clinical intake note using a Mad Studies lens",
  "input": "Patient presents with treatment-resistant depression and non-compliance with medication.",
  "output": "This person is navigating depression that hasn't responded to the medications tried so far. They've made autonomous decisions about their medication use that differ from what was prescribed."
}
```

Each pair says: *given this instruction (and optional input), here's what a good response looks like.* You're essentially showing the model hundreds or thousands of examples of the kind of responses you want it to produce.

For preference tuning (DPO/RLHF), you use a different format: the same prompt with a **chosen** (good) and **rejected** (bad) response, so the model learns to prefer one style over another.

## Resource 1: Unsloth — Fine-Tuning LLMs Guide

**URL:** https://unsloth.ai/docs/get-started/fine-tuning-llms-guide

**Summary:** Unsloth is one of the most popular open-source tools for LoRA/QLoRA fine-tuning, and their documentation is the closest thing to a "start here" guide for beginners. The key practical advice: your dataset should be structured as question-answer pairs (two columns), and quality matters far more than quantity. They offer a synthetic dataset notebook that can automatically parse documents (PDFs, videos, etc.), generate QA pairs, and auto-clean data using local models like Llama 3.2. Their docs walk through the full workflow from dataset creation to training, using the Alpaca format as the default. This is probably the single most useful starting point for someone who wants to actually *do* the thing, not just read about it.

**Why it's useful for this project:** Unsloth is likely the tool your researcher will actually use for training. Starting with their docs means the dataset format will be compatible from the start. The synthetic data generation notebook is especially relevant since creating hundreds of training pairs by hand is brutal.

## Resource 2: How to Create a Custom Alpaca Instruction Dataset

**URL:** https://zackproser.com/blog/how-to-create-a-custom-alpaca-dataset

**Summary:** A step-by-step tutorial that walks through creating a custom dataset in Alpaca format (instruction / input / output) stored as JSONL. The workflow is: (1) gather your source material, (2) write processing functions to convert it into the three-field structure, (3) generate the dataset by looping through your content, (4) optionally publish to Hugging Face. The tutorial uses a Jupyter Notebook, which makes it very accessible. It emphasizes that incorrect formatting will crash training runs, so getting the structure right matters. Includes a companion notebook you can clone and adapt.

**Why it's useful for this project:** This is the most hands-on, "here's exactly how to do it" tutorial in this list. Your researcher can adapt the workflow directly — instead of MDX blog posts as source material, she'd use her own curated mental health / disability theology content. The Jupyter Notebook format is beginner-friendly.

## Resource 4: Label Your Data — Synthetic Data for LLM Fine-Tuning

**URL:** https://labelyourdata.com/articles/llm-fine-tuning/synthetic-data

**Summary:** A practical overview of using synthetic data (generated by LLMs) to create training datasets. Covers three main approaches: (1) using generative models to create realistic samples, (2) distillation — having a larger "teacher" model create training examples for a smaller "student" model, and (3) model-in-the-loop approaches with continuous feedback. The quality control section is especially useful: they recommend dual-layer validation combining automated statistical checks with human-in-the-loop review, plus comparing synthetic data against real examples to check distribution alignment. Warns about model collapse (training on synthetic data that degrades over iterations) and bias amplification.

**Why it's useful for this project:** The distillation approach is directly relevant — your researcher could use Claude or GPT-4 to generate draft training pairs, then have domain experts (clinicians, people with lived experience, disability scholars) review and refine them. The quality control framework gives a concrete process for ensuring the synthetic data actually reflects the values and perspectives she wants the model to learn. The warnings about bias amplification are especially important for mental health/disability content.

## Resource 5: Fast Data Science — How to Fine-Tune an LLM for Mental Health Data

**URL:** https://fastdatascience.com/generative-ai/train-ai-fine-tune-ai/

**Summary:** One of the few resources specifically about fine-tuning LLMs for mental health applications. It highlights a critical problem: generalist models conflate mental health concepts that are actually distinct (e.g., "child bullies others" vs. "child is often bullied," or "I have trouble sleeping" vs. "I sleep very deeply"). The training data format they describe uses sentence pairs with human-rated similarity scores, annotated by psychologists. They recommend HuggingFace tutorials and Google Colab for implementation. The key takeaway: domain-expert annotation is essential because language nuances in mental health contexts are exactly where general-purpose models fail.

**Why it's useful for this project:** This is the most directly relevant resource to the Mad & Crip Theology project. It demonstrates *why* domain-specific fine-tuning matters (general models get mental health language wrong in predictable ways) and shows that expert-annotated data is what makes the difference. Your researcher's clinical training at CAMH and theology expertise are exactly the kind of domain knowledge that makes training data valuable.

## Resource 7: Stanford Alpaca & Self-Instruct Method (Original + Explainer)

**URL (Original):** https://crfm.stanford.edu/2023/03/13/alpaca.html
**URL (Explainer):** https://medium.com/@okanyenigun/self-instruct-style-data-generation-the-secret-behind-stanford-alpaca-e1575ea9ad71

**Summary:** Stanford's Alpaca project is the foundational example of generating synthetic instruction-tuning data. They started with just 175 human-written seed examples, then used an LLM to generate 52,000 instruction-following pairs for under $500. The Self-Instruct method is the core technique: you write a small number of high-quality seed examples that represent your desired style and domain, then prompt an LLM to generate more examples following the same patterns. The Medium explainer breaks down the process in accessible language. The key insight is that a small set of carefully crafted seeds can bootstrap a much larger dataset.

**Why it's useful for this project:** The Self-Instruct method is probably the most practical approach for this project. Your researcher could write 50–100 high-quality seed pairs that demonstrate responses grounded in Mad Studies, disability justice, and crip theology, then use Claude or another LLM to generate hundreds more. The seeds encode the values and perspective; the LLM scales the volume. This is also the method Unsloth's synthetic data notebook builds on.

## Resource 8: LoRA & QLoRA Fine-Tuning — Best Practices, Setup & Pitfalls

**URL:** https://medium.com/@QuarkAndCode/lora-qlora-llm-fine-tuning-best-practices-setup-pitfalls-c8147d34a6fd

**Summary:** A comprehensive guide covering the full LoRA/QLoRA fine-tuning process with heavy emphasis on data quality. Key data-related advice: (1) every data point must follow the same schema — inconsistent formatting is a top cause of poor results, (2) remove duplicate or near-duplicate examples to prevent overfitting, (3) always canonicalize your chat template (system/role names, special tokens) because mismatched templates degrade performance, (4) implement quality assurance with multiple reviewers checking the same data for consistency, (5) 1,000 high-quality examples beat 100,000 mediocre ones. Also covers hyperparameter selection and common technical pitfalls.

**Why it's useful for this project:** This is the "avoid the landmines" guide. Many first-time fine-tuners waste weeks on training only to discover their data was formatted inconsistently or had too many duplicates. The emphasis on quality over quantity is especially relevant — your researcher doesn't need to create 50,000 pairs. A carefully curated set of 500–2,000 high-quality pairs, consistently formatted and reviewed by people with the right expertise, will produce much better results.

## Suggested Workflow for This Project

Based on these resources, here's a practical path:

1. **Learn the format:** Read the Unsloth guide and the Zack Proser tutorial to understand the Alpaca format (instruction / input / output as JSONL).

2. **Study the precedent:** Read the MentalQLM preprint to see how someone built a similar mental health LoRA model and structured their training data.

3. **Write seed examples:** Create 50–100 high-quality training pairs by hand that demonstrate the kind of responses you want — grounded in Mad Studies, disability justice, crip theology, and lived experience. These are your seeds.

4. **Generate synthetic data:** Use the Self-Instruct method (via Unsloth's notebook or manually with Claude/GPT-4) to expand your seed set to 500–2,000 pairs.

5. **Quality review:** Have domain experts (clinicians, people with lived experience, disability scholars) review and correct the synthetic pairs. Use the dual-layer validation approach from Label Your Data.

6. **Clean and deduplicate:** Follow the pitfalls guide to remove duplicates, standardize formatting, and ensure consistency.

7. **Train and evaluate:** Use Unsloth with LoRA/QLoRA to fine-tune your chosen base model on the curated dataset.
