## Supervisor

Those are really thoughtful questions. 

You're right that it's primarily about showing engagement - proving you were actually present with their words instead of just waiting for your turn to talk. When someone feels truly heard, it often opens up space for them to go deeper or feel safer sharing more.

And yes - even when someone says something painful or negative, you still reflect back their actual words. Here's why: if someone says "I'm a failure at everything," and you reflect back "You're feeling like you're failing at everything right now," you're not agreeing that they ARE a failure. You're showing you heard their experience without trying to fix or argue with it yet. 

If you immediately jumped to "Oh, you're not a failure!" you'd be skipping over their actual feeling. They need to know you can sit with what they said before you offer anything else.

The only real exception might be if someone says something that puts them or others in immediate danger - but even then, you can often reflect first: "You're saying you want to hurt yourself tonight" before moving to safety responses.

Does that make sense? What comes up for you when you think about staying with someone's difficult words instead of immediately trying to make them feel better?
